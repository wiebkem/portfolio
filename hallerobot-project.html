Hallerobot

Project
Tangible Game Design as part of the course Children's Technology at Chalmers University

Target user
Preschool children

Timeframe
3 weeks in 2017

Role
UI/UX Designer, Researcher

Projectmembers
Wiebke Meyer, Jan Urbanek, Anukrit Verma and Zihua Yang

Website
Final prototype is available on Github.

Background

Children today use technology fluently and naturally in many parts of their lives. But children are also far detached from the inner workings of technology, these are hidden away behind nice usable interfaces. A new wave of games that teach children programming have been hitting the market in the past 10 years and new visual programming tools are emerging, with the aim to reintroduce programming to the wider public. Programming is not something that is part of our natural upbringing, such as writing and painting. In this project we tried to deal with the the possible solution to the problem stated above and we worked with 3-4 age children designing a logic game.

Design Process

Before we started with the design, we visited the preschool and discussed the project purpose with the teacher. Additionally, we observed the environment and the children and planned three workshops with them.
After the first impression we brainstormed and looked for existing games and technologies. As a group we had an ideation session and worked out a prototype (based on Wizard of Oz) to test it in the first workshop with the children. During that workshop we observed the children's behavior and got feedback from the teacher as well.
In the second workshop we tested our improved prototype and carried out a codesign session in which the children were allowed to draw their own robots.
The third workshop included our final design and a thank you certificate for all children. To get some valuable and constructive feedback we used structured observation methods and had a final debrief dialogue with the teacher and the children.

Final design

All in all, we ended up with a 3x3 grid as shown in figure 1. The robot always shows up in the lower left grid, facing upwards, and can be moved with the arrows.

The facing direction (image) of the robot can be changed by using the keyboard buttons 'a' (figure 2), 'w' (figure 3), 's' (figure \4) and 'd' (shown within figure 1).
The gift is randomly positioned on a square in the grid, but never on or next to the robot. The robot is moved by a facilitator, for example a teacher, based on the arrows the children have put on the game board (figure 5).

Once the robot is positioned on the same square as the gift, the teachers is supposed to click on 'enter' to start the applause and animation. The applause is played for 10 seconds and afterwards an animated robot with happy background music is shown to the children. The animated robots are robots that the children have drawn themselves, like in figure 6.
